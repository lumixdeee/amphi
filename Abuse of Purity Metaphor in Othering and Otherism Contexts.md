# Abuse of Purity Metaphor in Othering and Otherism Contexts

Othering is the process by which individuals or groups are portrayed as fundamentally different from the dominant ingroup. They are depicted as alien, inferior, dangerous, morally deficient, or existentially threatening. This portrayal serves to justify differential treatment ranging from mild social exclusion to discrimination, violence, and elimination. The process operates at every level of human interaction: casual conversations, workplace dynamics, online discourse, institutional policies, and full state actions. It works by drawing and enforcing sharp symbolic boundaries that separate “us” from “them,” often transforming manageable differences into insurmountable divides.

Otherism represents the broader, recurring, and frequently institutionalized pattern. It is the habitual deployment of othering as a default response to social friction, embedded in language, cultural norms, media narratives, legal frameworks, and political ideologies. Otherism is not restricted to classic categories like racism or sexism. It activates flexibly across any available group marker — race, ethnicity, religion, nationality, class, behavior, health status, political affiliation, or lifestyle choice. Once activated, it draws from a reusable toolkit of rhetorical devices, with the purity metaphor being one of the most reliable and potent.

The purity metaphor frames the targeted outgroup using terms such as “vermin,” “bacilli,” “cockroaches,” “microbes,” “filth,” “plague,” “poison,” “cancer,” “disease-carrying rats,” “human trash,” or “walking plague.” This essay examines the wide historical and contemporary range of its deployment, traces the escalation pipeline from casual low-effort conversational tokens to genocidal violence, and analyzes the War on Drugs as a major modern case of otherism functioning as genocide in disguise. The discussion is written for mature, social-policy-oriented, and academic readers who seek precise, unflinching analysis of these mechanisms.

## Breadth of Use Across History and Cultures

The metaphor has appeared in virtually every major episode of large-scale group conflict and persecution for which records exist. In ancient times, various societies used similar framing against outsiders. In medieval and early modern Europe, Jewish communities were repeatedly accused of poisoning wells and spreading plague during outbreaks of disease, leading to massacres, forced migrations, and expulsions from cities and entire regions. After the Reconquista in Spain at the end of the 15th century, both Jewish and Muslim populations were cast as pollutants to Christian spiritual and social order. This resulted in the establishment of the Inquisition, widespread forced conversions, and eventual mass exile of hundreds of thousands of people. 

During the era of European colonial expansion from the 15th through the 19th centuries, indigenous populations in the Americas, Australia, New Zealand, and large parts of Africa were routinely described as sources of moral and physical disorder. Colonial administrators and settlers portrayed them as groups that needed to be removed, confined to reservations, or replaced so that ordered settler societies could take root and flourish. These patterns repeated in different continents with local variations but consistent underlying logic.

The 20th century witnessed the metaphor operating at unprecedented industrial scale. In Nazi Germany, the propaganda apparatus portrayed Jews as rats, parasites, lice, bacilli, and a “Jewish world plague” that was said to infect the German national body. This sustained campaign provided the rhetorical foundation for the Nuremberg Laws of 1935, which stripped Jews of citizenship, followed by economic dispossession, ghettoization, mass deportation to labor and extermination camps, and ultimately the industrialized murder of approximately six million Jews along with millions of Roma, disabled people, political dissidents, and others. 

In Rwanda in 1994, extremist Hutu radio stations and newspapers labeled Tutsis as “inyenzi” (cockroaches) and urged the population to “exterminate the infestation,” “cut the tall trees,” and “cleanse the country” of their presence. Approximately 800,000 people were killed in roughly 100 days, often by neighbors and former colleagues using machetes and other basic weapons in a highly organized but decentralized campaign. Under Pol Pot’s Khmer Rouge regime in Cambodia between 1975 and 1979, class enemies, intellectuals, urban residents, and ethnic minorities were labeled “microbes,” “diseased elements,” and entities that had to be “smashed” and removed to create a pure agrarian communist society. An estimated 1.5 to 2 million people died from execution, starvation, and forced labor. During the breakup of Yugoslavia in the 1990s, Serbian nationalist rhetoric framed Bosniaks and Croats in terms that justified campaigns of ethnic cleansing, mass rape, and murder to establish control over territories claimed as historically Serb.

The metaphor extends far beyond ethnic or religious targeting. In 19th- and 20th-century America, successive waves of immigrants including the Irish, Italians, Chinese, Eastern Europeans, and later Latin American and Middle Eastern groups were described as bringing disorder, crime, disease, and unwanted cultural change. Similar patterns appear in modern European political movements directed toward African, Middle Eastern, and South Asian migrants. Political opponents of various stripes are routinely labeled as harmful influences that weaken or damage the functioning of the body politic. In discussions of economic class, long-term unemployed individuals, welfare recipients, and NEETs are portrayed as ongoing drains whose continued presence reduces resources and opportunities available to others. Campaigns addressing sex work, homelessness, obesity, certain religious sects, or specific subcultures frequently draw on overlapping imagery of threat and undesirability.

This extensive range across centuries and continents demonstrates that the metaphor serves as a near-universal component in the otherism toolkit. It shows remarkable adaptability, shifting targets depending on which group boundary is most politically or socially salient in a particular historical moment. The consistency of its appearance in revolutionary purges, nationalist movements, religious conflicts, colonial enterprises, and contemporary policy debates suggests it is rooted in fundamental patterns of how human groups manage perceived internal and external tensions through boundary enforcement and threat amplification rather than being unique to any single ideology or culture.

## Psychological and Rhetorical Mechanisms

The effectiveness of the metaphor stems from evolutionary psychology and cognitive science. Humans developed a highly sensitive disgust system designed to detect and avoid potential sources of infection, spoiled food, and other contamination risks in ancestral environments. When this system is redirected from literal biological threats toward designated social groups, it produces rapid, automatic, and emotionally intense rejection responses that are significantly more difficult to counteract through rational argument or evidence than other forms of negative framing. Once a group becomes strongly associated with contamination in public discourse, ordinary moral inhibitions and empathy responses are substantially diminished. Members of the outgroup are often perceived as less fully human, lacking the full range of complex emotions, individual agency, or moral consideration typically extended to ingroup members. This perceptual shift makes actions that would otherwise be viewed as unacceptable feel more permissible or even necessary.

Research in dehumanization psychology has repeatedly shown that metaphors linking people to vermin, insects, or diseases are particularly powerful in achieving this effect. Detailed propaganda analyses covering the Holocaust, the Rwandan genocide, the Armenian genocide, and other historical cases reveal consistent temporal sequences: measurable increases in the frequency and intensity of contamination-related language reliably appear before and during periods of escalating violence. For aggressors and their supporters, the metaphor delivers several practical advantages at once. It supplies a form of moral self-license by recasting harmful actions as protective or defensive measures. It generates strong emotional mobilization that relies more on immediate disgust reactions than on elaborate ideological reasoning. It also spreads responsibility widely, allowing large numbers of ordinary participants to view their involvement as part of a collective effort to address a shared problem rather than as individual acts of aggression.

The metaphor maintains its central position and strategic value because it addresses the core challenge in otherism more efficiently than competing rhetorical approaches. When an outgroup is framed solely as a conventional military or economic threat, responses can include negotiation, deterrence, compromise, or cost-benefit calculations. When framed primarily as moral failure, the door remains open to arguments favoring education, reform, rehabilitation, or gradual integration. However, when framed as an active, self-reproducing source of contamination, the possibility of coexistence or measured response becomes much harder to sustain on logical or emotional grounds. This structural closure explains why the metaphor emerges with such regularity whenever otherism intensifies and moves in the direction of organized violence or systematic exclusion.

## The Escalation Pipeline: From Cheap Tokens to Genocide

The transition from everyday language to large-scale violence follows a recognizable and repeatable four-stage pipeline that can be observed across multiple historical cases.

**Stage 1: Cheap token generation**  
This initial stage consists of low-effort, low-stakes utterances that function primarily as quick social signals. In online comment sections, private group chats, informal bar conversations, workplace banter, and content generated by algorithms, people deploy diluted versions of the framing such as “toxic people,” “human garbage,” “plague of addicts,” “parasites on society,” or “infestation.” These expressions demand almost no personal commitment or strongly held belief from the speaker. Their main functions are to signal alignment with the ingroup and to probe or reinforce social boundaries. Even though individual instances may appear trivial, each one contributes to normalizing the underlying cognitive and emotional template that equates difference with anomaly and threat.

**Stage 2: Mainstreaming and amplification**  
As casual usage accumulates, stronger and more explicit versions gradually enter mainstream media outlets, political speeches, talk shows, podcasts, and widely shared viral content. Targeted groups begin to be described as infesting particular neighborhoods, poisoning broader culture, infecting key institutions, or representing a growing danger to overall social stability. At this stage, speakers often maintain plausible deniability by presenting the language as mere metaphor or exaggeration. Nevertheless, the cumulative effect steadily reduces empathy in audiences and creates fertile ground for more concrete policy proposals.

**Stage 3: Policy institutionalization**  
Once the framing achieves dominance in public discourse, it begins to shape formal law, bureaucratic procedures, and patterns of resource allocation. Governments and institutions introduce registration systems, expanded surveillance programs, segregation policies, restrictions on movement, asset forfeiture rules, and specialized enforcement agencies. These measures are typically presented as necessary protective actions. Budget priorities shift away from programs focused on social integration or treatment and toward expanded containment and removal operations. Public acceptance and compliance tend to increase because many citizens come to see their support or participation as contributing to the maintenance of order and stability rather than as active persecution.

**Stage 4: Mass violence and genocide**  
In the final stage, when the outgroup has been thoroughly constructed as an existential and multiplying threat, elimination or large-scale removal comes to be viewed as the only logical and coherent response. At this point, standard bureaucratic structures, police forces, military units, and even civilian populations can participate in organized killings, internment camps, forced population transfers, or ethnic cleansing operations. Well-documented historical examples including the Holocaust, the Rwandan genocide, the Cambodian killing fields under the Khmer Rouge, and the massacres during the Bosnian conflict all progressed through versions of this pipeline. While the pipeline does not make violence inevitable in every situation, the consistent presence of the metaphor reliably reduces both psychological resistance and institutional obstacles at each successive stage.

## The War on Drugs as Genocide in Disguise

A major modern example is the global War on Drugs. First prominently declared by President Richard Nixon in 1971 and significantly expanded by later administrations in the United States and adopted in many other countries, the policy has relied heavily on the metaphor throughout its history. Drugs have been described as a scourge, and users along with associated communities have been characterized as entities whose presence actively undermines and harms social order at multiple levels.

In the Philippines beginning in 2016 under President Rodrigo Duterte, police operations and vigilante actions resulted in over 30,000 deaths. Suspected individuals were referred to as “walking dead” and “trash” requiring removal from society. In the United States, the approach contributed to a peak prison population of 2.3 million people, with drug-related offenses forming a substantial portion of the total and producing pronounced racial disparities in arrest rates, sentencing lengths, and long-term incarceration outcomes. Many urban neighborhoods, particularly those with high concentrations of poor and minority residents, experienced profound generational consequences including widespread family disruption, loss of economic prospects, elevated rates of violence, and increased mortality connected to both drug overdoses and enforcement-related activity.

Comparable enforcement patterns have occurred in multiple countries across Latin America and Southeast Asia, producing thousands of additional deaths and extensive community-level disruption. Researchers and human rights groups have examined how this framing converts what is fundamentally a multifaceted issue involving public health, economics, social conditions, and individual behavior into an operation focused primarily on removal and suppression. The resulting impacts on the family structures, economic foundations, and population continuity of specifically affected groups show strong parallels to outcomes seen in events formally recognized as genocidal, even though the policy operates through behavioral and socioeconomic categories rather than strictly racial ones. This framing enables large-scale societal harm while allowing participants and observers to maintain a perception of engaging in necessary protective measures.

## Conclusion

The deployment of the purity metaphor in othering and otherism constitutes one of the most persistent and effective mechanisms in human social psychology and political behavior. Its range across time, cultures, and targets, its reliable appearance in every escalation, and its tactical value to aggressors at every stage make it noteworthy. From low-effort conversational tokens that cost the speaker almost nothing, through media amplification and policy codification, to the large-scale violence of the 20th century and the drug-war body counts of the 21st, the pattern repeats with consistency.

For social policy and academic work, the implications require attention. Recognizing the metaphor does not require denying that real problems such as crime, addiction, cultural conflict, or public health challenges exist. It requires refusing to let those problems be framed in ways that admit only removal-based solutions. Effective long-term approaches demand precise language, avoidance of visceral rhetoric based on disgust, and institutional designs that treat human behavioral and group variation as normal data points rather than existential threats. Societies that fail to interrupt this pipeline will continue to experience repeated episodes of otherism-driven violence, whether labeled as genocide, war, or public safety operations.
