# SCH — Why CAN-PSY Looks Real When CAN-PSY Is Not One Object

## Abstract

CAN-PSY names the compressed public pair:

```text
cannabis -> psychosis
```

This paper argues that the pair can look persuasive while failing as one causal object.

The association may survive. Some cannabis exposure states may matter. THC can alter salience, threat interpretation, anxiety, perception, sleep, reward, and social behaviour. Frequent high-THC use, early heavy use, acute intoxication, continued use after onset, and cannabis use disorder labels may each mark higher-risk strata in different ways.

The failure is not “no relation.” The failure is route custody.

“Cannabis” is not one exposure. “Psychosis” is not one endpoint. Cannabis use disorder is not molecular dose. Service contact is not incidence. Enforcement is not background. Diagnosis is not neutral visibility. Warning language is not mechanism. Record order is not always life order. Other substances do not vanish because the cannabis field is easier to publish, easier to code, or less socially staining than LSD, stimulants, alcohol, sedatives, tobacco, synthetic cannabinoids, or polysubstance use. Peak valence, relief, romance, status shift, grief release, social reward, party rhythm, identity reorganization, and sleep disruption are often missing from ordinary risk instruments.

CAN-PSY looks real because many real routes feed the same pair-name. CAN-PSY is not one object because those routes do not pass through one gate.

The repair is not to deny the association. The repair is to make every claim name its route:

```text
Which product?
Which dose?
Which route of administration?
Which THC/CBD profile?
Which person-state?
Which sleep state?
Which co-substances?
Which prior symptoms?
Which law/contact field?
Which service route?
Which endpoint?
Which comparison group?
Which claim size?
```

Core keeper:

```text
A bounded route can earn trust.
The public arrow cannot inherit the whole field.
```

## 0. What v0.006 repairs

The outside review accepted the main v0.005 move: the papers had crossed from route-map critique into worked audit instrument. It also named the next weakness.

v0.005 still leaned on stylized audits. v0.006 therefore adds four repairs:

```text
1. Dual scoring:
   score the single CAN-PSY object separately from bounded route claims.

2. Co-substance attribution custody:
   do not let cannabis absorb LSD, stimulants, alcohol, sedatives, tobacco,
   synthetic cannabinoids, medication effects, withdrawal, or unknown street mixtures.

3. Third audit lane:
   add a longitudinal / genetically informed study type to the registry and
   FEP case-control audits.

4. Claim-size rent:
   a bounded warning does not pay the same rent as a total public causal object.
```

The review is useful friction, not authority. It can force the paper to move, but it cannot push the paper back into the public arrow unless the route earns that claim.

## 1. One-page answer

Why does CAN-PSY look real when CAN-PSY is not one object?

Because multiple real mechanisms, biases, and routes point into the same two labels.

```text
pharmacological route:
  THC exposure, high potency, low CBD, acute intoxication, sleep disruption,
  anxiety, salience, threat interpretation, reward, perception

developmental / vulnerability route:
  age, prior symptoms, family liability, trauma, neurodevelopment, stress,
  social defeat, deprivation, and earlier distress

reverse-path route:
  emerging symptoms, insomnia, arousal, boredom, terror, relief-seeking,
  identity instability, or high-valence activation may precede or shape use

co-substance route:
  alcohol, nicotine, stimulants, LSD, MDMA, ketamine, benzodiazepines,
  synthetic cannabinoids, opioids, medication changes, and withdrawal may enter
  the event but disappear under the cannabis label

service-contact route:
  ED presentation, hospital admission, police contact, referral, clinician
  suspicion, family reporting, and CUD coding change who becomes visible

law / enforcement route:
  illegality and selective policing alter searching, recording, fear,
  disclosure, witness statements, and dataset entry

diagnostic route:
  symptoms, admissions, crisis narratives, and service histories become codes

culture-loop route:
  warnings, media, funding questions, legal debate, stigma, and social scripts
  teach people how to notice, report, fear, deny, or over-attribute the pair

peak-valence route:
  wins, recognition, romance, celebration, sudden belonging, creative breakthrough,
  relief, party rhythm, and sleep loss can make cannabis appear near onset
  without owning the whole route
```

Each route can be real. The mistake is letting one public arrow inherit all routes.

The right sentence is not:

```text
cannabis causes psychosis
```

The better scientific object is:

```text
Which cannabis exposure state, in which person-state, through which route,
inside which contact field, is associated with which psychosis-related endpoint,
relative to which comparator, before which warning or policy claim is made?
```

## 2. Claim-status ladder

The same evidence can support different claim sizes. v0.006 uses five levels.

| Level | Claim | Rent required |
|---:|---|---|
| 1 | Cannabis labels and psychosis labels co-occur in some records. | Basic association design and timing. |
| 2 | Specific cannabis exposure states are associated with psychosis-related outcomes. | Product/frequency/route/endpoints named; confounds handled. |
| 3 | A bounded product-route is plausible or well supported in a subgroup. | Product-level exposure, prior-state measurement, co-substance custody, and comparison group discipline. |
| 4 | A causal route is strong enough for targeted warning or intervention. | Longitudinal timing, dose/route specificity, sensitivity checks, state/context controls, competing-route accounting. |
| 5 | The public arrow “cannabis causes psychosis” is licensed as one causal object. | One exposure, one endpoint, one mechanism, and low residual route ambiguity. This level is not earned by the ordinary CAN-PSY field. |

Most public discourse jumps from Level 1 or 2 to Level 5. The repair is not silence. The repair is level-matched speech.

## 3. Evidence-type ladder

A study does not become false because it fails the total-object gate. It may still support a bounded warning.

| Evidence type | What it can support | What it cannot carry alone |
|---|---|---|
| Acute intoxication report | Short-window cannabis-related symptoms in a context | Lifetime causal story |
| Experimental THC challenge | Pharmacological plausibility under controlled exposure | Street-market, long-term, service-contact, or diagnosis pathway |
| Case-control FEP study | Association between reported exposure and FEP presentation | Population incidence, all psychosis, all cannabis, all routes |
| Registry CUD study | Contact-field association between CUD-coded presentation and later diagnosis | Product-dose molecular exposure |
| Longitudinal cohort | Temporal ordering and some confound reduction | Full custody of product, route, disclosure, and unmeasured state |
| Twin / sibling / genetic design | Some shared-liability pressure | Full replacement of product, service-contact, and culture-loop gates |
| Media / warning study | Culture-loop effect | Chemical mechanism |
| Qualitative case file | Route discovery and missing-variable detection | Population-level claim |

The ladder’s point is not to demote every study. It is to stop one evidence type from wearing another evidence type’s authority.

## 4. Three routes not to fuse

A recurring fault in CAN-PSY is route fusion.

### 4.1 Chemical route

A chemical route asks whether specific cannabinoid exposure states contribute to psychosis-related symptoms or presentations.

It needs:

```text
product composition;
THC/CBD ratio;
dose;
route;
frequency;
tolerance;
age;
sleep;
prior symptoms;
co-substances;
time from exposure to symptom;
symptom type;
comparator.
```

This route may win bounded claims. It is strongest where product and timing are named and acute or near-term effects are measured.

### 4.2 Enforcement / contact route

An enforcement/contact route asks whether law, policing, fear, stigma, emergency contact, hospital pathway, disclosure pressure, and diagnosis coding alter who appears in records.

It needs:

```text
search/contact data;
arrest or police-contact route;
service-contact timing;
family/witness reporting;
clinician expectation;
CUD coding path;
policy period;
social sorting;
race/class/geography controls;
access to care.
```

This route does not mean chemistry is irrelevant. It means the record is not neutral exposure.

### 4.3 Chemical route inside enforcement/contact field

Many real cases are mixed.

A person may use high-THC cannabis, sleep badly, become frightened, be seen by police or family, present to services, receive a CUD code, and later receive a schizophrenia-spectrum diagnosis. That is not “chemical only” and not “policing only.” It is a route chain.

The study claim must match the chain it actually measured.

## 5. Co-substance attribution custody

v0.006 adds this section because the review pack forced it. The cannabis label often becomes the socially available container.

A person may have used:

```text
LSD;
psilocybin;
MDMA;
cocaine;
amphetamine or methamphetamine;
ketamine;
alcohol;
benzodiazepines;
opioids;
synthetic cannabinoids;
high-nicotine products;
prescribed or stopped medication;
sleep aids;
unknown street mixtures.
```

The record may still say cannabis.

Reasons:

```text
cannabis is easier to disclose;
LSD or stimulant use may carry heavier identity, family, legal, employment,
or clinical consequences;
witnesses may know about cannabis but not other use;
urine screens may miss, mistime, or overrepresent different substances;
cannabis metabolites persist longer than many substances;
clinicians may code the known or admitted substance;
families may narrate the less socially destructive substance;
the person may not know what was in the product;
some substances may be permanent stains in the story while cannabis is the
negotiable explanation.
```

Therefore every CAN-PSY audit needs a co-substance gate.

```text
OTHER_SUBSTANCE_GATE = {
  toxicology window;
  self-report window;
  witness-report window;
  substance stigma gradient;
  disclosure risk;
  metabolite persistence;
  street-product uncertainty;
  medication start/stop;
  alcohol and nicotine load;
  stimulant and psychedelic exposure;
  synthetic cannabinoid possibility;
}
```

A cannabis-coded event with poor co-substance custody can still support a contact-field or crisis-route claim. It cannot carry a product-level cannabinoid claim by itself.

## 6. Peak valence as gate, not crown

Peak valence means high-positive or high-arousal life states that can precede, accompany, or amplify crisis:

```text
unexpected win;
recognition;
romance;
sexual attention;
status shift;
creative breakthrough;
religious or symbolic uplift;
party rhythm;
social belonging;
relief after fear;
sudden freedom;
sleep compression.
```

Cannabis can appear near such states because people use during celebration, social bonding, parties, creative flow, relief, or identity transition. If a study only records cannabis before diagnosis, the person-state route can be misfiled as plant-cause.

Peak valence is not a replacement explanation. It is a missing gate:

```text
Was cannabis the active route?
Was cannabis a marker of the state?
Was cannabis used after the state began?
Was cannabis part of a mixed sleep/social/substance field?
Did the high-valence event alter witnesses, family concern, or service contact?
```

The gate matters because an onset story can be routed through the most visible object rather than the most causal route.

## 7. Dual scoring method

v0.006 uses two scores for each study type.

```text
Score A:
  Single CAN-PSY object trust.
  Does this evidence license the total public arrow?

Score B:
  Bounded route trust.
  Does this evidence support a named route with limited wording?
```

Scores are not measurements of truth. They are audit scaffolds. They force the paper to say what survives and what does not.

Suggested scale:

```text
0-5:
  weak for the claim

6-10:
  association or route hint

11-15:
  serious bounded route support

16-20:
  strong bounded route support under current design

20:
  reserved for a claim whose exposure, endpoint, timing, confound,
  contact, and route custody all match the sentence being made
```

## 8. Worked audit 1: FEP case-control high-potency / daily-use study type

Model: a multi-site first-episode psychosis case-control study reporting higher odds of FEP among daily users and especially among daily high-potency users.

### What survives

```text
A serious bounded association:
  daily use and high-potency reported exposure appear in excess among FEP cases
  relative to controls in the sampled settings.

A plausible product-route warning:
  frequent high-THC use may increase risk of psychosis-related presentation
  in vulnerable or high-exposure groups.

A public-health reason to measure potency:
  plant-label cannabis is too crude for risk claims.
```

### What does not survive

```text
The total public arrow:
  cannabis -> psychosis

A one-product claim:
  "cannabis" remains heterogeneous.

A one-outcome claim:
  FEP presentation is not all psychosis incidence.

A no-confound claim:
  reverse path, sleep, distress, co-drugs, tobacco, alcohol, social defeat,
  deprivation, service access, and recruitment pathways still matter.

A no-contact claim:
  help-seeking and clinical routing affect who becomes a case.
```

### Co-substance check

The case-control study type must ask:

```text
Were stimulants, psychedelics, alcohol, tobacco, synthetic cannabinoids,
medication changes, and withdrawal measured with timing?
Were they adjusted as confounds, mediators, or competing routes?
Was cannabis easier to disclose than LSD or stimulant use?
Were toxicology windows aligned with symptom onset?
```

### Scores

```text
Score A: single CAN-PSY object trust = 10 / 20
Score B: bounded high-potency / daily-use FEP route trust = 16 / 20
```

Interpretation:

The study type can support targeted warnings about daily high-potency exposure. It does not license the whole public arrow.

## 9. Worked audit 2: registry CUD / schizophrenia-diagnosis study type

Model: a large health-administrative registry study reporting that people with emergency or hospital-coded cannabis use disorder have higher subsequent schizophrenia-spectrum diagnosis rates, and that the population-attributable fraction changes across policy periods.

### What survives

```text
A strong contact-field association:
  a CUD-coded emergency or hospital contact is a serious marker of later
  schizophrenia-spectrum diagnosis in that record system.

A service-risk warning:
  people presenting with cannabis-related disorder labels deserve follow-up,
  sleep support, distress care, substance assessment, and early psychosis attention.

A policy-period signal:
  legal, market, product, service, coding, and help-seeking shifts may change
  what the registry sees.
```

### What does not survive

```text
Product-level cannabinoid dose:
  CUD code is not THC/CBD exposure.

Population incidence:
  diagnosis records are not all community events.

One-route causality:
  CUD code may reflect severity, distress, service contact, co-substances,
  prior symptoms, social risk, and clinical attention.

Legalization as chemistry:
  policy period is not molecular exposure.
```

### Co-substance check

Registry CUD studies are especially vulnerable to stain-routing.

```text
Does the record contain alcohol disorder?
stimulant disorder?
psychedelic exposure?
synthetic cannabinoid exposure?
tobacco dependence?
medication changes?
prior psychiatric contact?
ED toxicology?
police or family route?
```

If the record mainly captures the visible or admitted substance, cannabis may become the index label for a polysubstance or crisis-contact event.

### Scores

```text
Score A: single CAN-PSY object trust = 7 / 20
Score B: CUD/contact-field risk marker trust = 17 / 20
```

Interpretation:

The study type can support serious service follow-up for CUD-coded crisis contact. It cannot turn CUD code into product-level cannabis exposure.

## 10. Worked audit 3: longitudinal / genetically informed study type

Model: a longitudinal, sibling, twin, polygenic, or Mendelian-style study that tries to separate cannabis exposure from shared liability and prior vulnerability.

### What survives

```text
Better pressure on reverse path and shared liability than ordinary cross-sectional
or case-control designs.

Potential evidence that cannabis exposure, cannabis use disorder, psychosis risk,
and genetic liability are entangled before diagnosis.

Useful separation between:
  exposure precedes outcome;
  liability predicts both exposure and outcome;
  early symptoms predict exposure;
  family/social context loads both sides.
```

### What does not survive

```text
Product-level route by default:
  many genetically informed designs still use broad use/ever-use/CUD labels.

Endpoint singularity:
  psychotic experiences, FEP, schizophrenia diagnosis, admission, and symptom
  scales are not interchangeable.

Co-substance custody by default:
  genetic or sibling designs do not automatically name LSD, stimulants, alcohol,
  nicotine, synthetic cannabinoids, medication changes, or withdrawal.

Culture/contact route by default:
  family, school, police, service, and disclosure routes can still affect
  measurement.

Peak-valence route by default:
  high-arousal person-states may remain unmeasured.
```

### Scores

```text
Score A: single CAN-PSY object trust = 9 / 20
Score B: shared-liability / exposure-route sorting trust = 15 / 20
```

Interpretation:

This study type can move the field by reducing some timing and liability ambiguity. It still does not rescue the total public arrow unless exposure and endpoint custody match the claim.

## 11. What would move this paper

The paper can move. It is not a permanent veto.

### Toward a stronger bounded chemical route

Evidence would move the score upward if it shows:

```text
product-level THC/CBD measurement;
route and dose measured before symptoms;
sleep and prior symptoms measured before use;
co-substances measured with timing;
repeated exposure-response within the same person or matched cohorts;
reduction after exposure change;
replication across settings with different enforcement/contact fields;
endpoint specificity;
low dependence on broad CUD or plant-label exposure.
```

### Toward a stronger enforcement/contact route

Evidence would move the score upward if it shows:

```text
same product exposure but different record entry under different policing,
service, disclosure, or warning conditions;
CUD coding shifts without matching product-dose shifts;
diagnosis/contact changes tracking policy, clinician expectation, or media scripts;
strong social sorting of who is searched, admitted, coded, or referred.
```

### Toward a stronger reverse-path or peak-valence route

Evidence would move the score upward if it shows:

```text
sleep disruption, salience shift, distress, relief-seeking, celebration,
status change, romance, creative activation, or fear emerging before use escalation;
cannabis used after person-state change rather than before it;
witness-field or family-response shifts around high-valence onset;
episode timing better predicted by arousal-state sequence than by product exposure alone.
```

### Toward granting the public arrow

The public arrow would require much more:

```text
specific exposure;
specific endpoint;
time order;
co-substance custody;
shared-liability pressure;
service/contact route handling;
law/media loop handling;
replication across settings;
bounded mechanism;
no route family carrying the sentence by proxy.
```

That is why this paper allows bounded warnings while blocking total inheritance.

## 12. Better surviving objects

Instead of CAN-PSY, use smaller objects.

| Surviving object | Usable wording |
|---|---|
| Acute THC symptom route | “High-THC exposure may contribute to acute paranoia, perceptual disturbance, panic, or salience instability in some settings.” |
| Daily high-potency FEP route | “Daily high-potency exposure is a serious candidate route for FEP presentation in some populations and deserves product-level measurement.” |
| CUD contact-field marker | “CUD-coded crisis contact marks a high-follow-up group; it is not the same as THC dose.” |
| Continued-use after onset route | “Continued high-THC use after psychosis-related symptoms may worsen course for some people.” |
| Withdrawal / sleep route | “Stopping, resuming, or changing use may affect sleep and arousal; this can matter near crisis.” |
| Co-substance crisis route | “Cannabis-coded crisis records need co-substance timing before product-route claims are made.” |
| Peak-valence route | “High-valence or high-arousal life events can place cannabis near onset without making cannabis the owner of the route.” |
| Contact/diagnosis route | “Law, service, diagnosis, and warning systems can alter which events become visible.” |

These objects are less catchy than the public arrow. They are more faithful to the evidence field.

## 13. Study design sketch

A better CAN-PSY study would not start with the pair-name. It would start with route custody.

Minimum fields:

```text
product:
  THC, CBD, minor cannabinoids if available, source, route, dose, frequency,
  tolerance, recent change, withdrawal, resumption

person-state:
  sleep, stress, trauma, social defeat, prior symptoms, family history,
  neurodevelopmental risk, medication changes, nutrition, hydration

co-substances:
  alcohol, nicotine, stimulants, psychedelics, synthetic cannabinoids,
  sedatives, opioids, medication start/stop, unknown mixtures, toxicology windows

valence / event field:
  wins, losses, romance, status shift, celebration, grief relief, party rhythm,
  creative activation, social belonging, spiritual/religious uplift

contact field:
  family concern, police contact, ED route, GP route, school/work route,
  clinician expectation, disclosure risk, diagnosis pathway

culture field:
  warning exposure, media exposure, stigma, legal status, local enforcement,
  social scripts, cannabis-psychosis belief before presentation

endpoint:
  acute symptoms, distress, help-seeking, FEP presentation, diagnosis code,
  admission, duration, recurrence, course
```

Analysis should produce separate estimates for:

```text
product-route association;
co-substance route association;
prior-state / reverse-path association;
contact-field association;
endpoint-specific association;
public-slogan permission.
```

The final category should usually be the lowest score.

## 14. Public wording

A useful public warning can be bounded without becoming the public arrow.

Better:

```text
Frequent high-THC use, especially daily use, early heavy use, use during sleep
loss or distress, or continued use after psychosis-related symptoms, may raise
risk for some people and deserves early support, product-specific warnings,
and non-punitive care.
```

Avoid:

```text
Cannabis causes psychosis.
```

Avoid also:

```text
Cannabis has nothing to do with psychosis.
```

The first sentence steals the field. The second throws away risk. The paper argues for a third sentence:

```text
Name the route, then name the warning.
```

## 15. Conclusion

CAN-PSY looks real because the association field is real enough to keep reappearing.

It is not one object because the field contains many routes:

```text
chemical route;
product route;
daily-use route;
CUD/contact route;
reverse-path route;
shared-liability route;
co-substance route;
sleep route;
peak-valence route;
law/enforcement route;
service-contact route;
diagnostic route;
warning/media route.
```

A bounded route can earn trust. A service warning can matter. A product-specific warning can matter. A person in crisis deserves support, not a lecture and not a slogan.

The final position:

```text
The association may survive.
The warning may matter.
The route must be named.
The public arrow cannot inherit the whole stack.
```
