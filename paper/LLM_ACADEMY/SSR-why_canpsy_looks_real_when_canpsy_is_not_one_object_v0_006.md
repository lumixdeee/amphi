# CAN-PSY Looks Real Because Routes Converge, Not Because One Object Has Been Found

## Cannabis, psychosis, CUD, co-drugs, enforcement, service contact, peak valence, and the object-custody test

**Version:** v0.006
**Date:** 2026-07-04
**Status:** working paper / object-custody instrument
**Boundary:** not medical advice, not legal advice, not a denial that cannabis-related harms occur

## Abstract

CAN-PSY names the compressed public pair:

```text
cannabis -> psychosis
```

The pair looks real because the claimed association has real feedstock. THC can alter perception, salience, anxiety, threat interpretation, sleep, appetite, reward, and meaning. Frequent use, high-potency use, early use, cannabis use disorder, continued use after onset, and acute cannabis-associated psychotic symptoms all deserve serious attention. Some cannabis exposure states may be harmful for some people in some conditions.

That does not make CAN-PSY one causal object.

The object fails because the two nouns are not enough. “Cannabis” may mean product chemistry, plant identity, self-report, law status, CUD code, service route, social label, or police-visible conduct. “Psychosis” may mean intoxication-linked paranoia, panic, psychotic-like experience, first episode psychosis, hospital contact, relapse, cannabis-induced psychotic disorder, schizophrenia-spectrum diagnosis, administrative code, family report, police route, or lived altered state. Cannabis use disorder is not molecular dose. Service contact is not incidence. Diagnosis code is not event rate. Illegality is not background. Other drugs are not decorative confounders. LSD, stimulants, alcohol, synthetic cannabinoids, tobacco, medication changes, withdrawal, and hidden co-use can enter the same record while cannabis inherits the visible blame.

The safer position is:

```text
claimed association != nothing
claimed association != single causal object
```

The null hypothesis remains:

```text
No single CAN-PSY object has earned trust until product, person-state,
time order, co-drugs, shared liability, law/contact field, service route,
diagnostic route, endpoint identity, and population movement are separated.
```

The paper does not ask whether cannabis is harmless. It asks which route is being claimed.

## 1. The claim split

The sentence “cannabis causes psychosis” can hide many different claims:

```text
A. Acute THC can produce paranoia, panic, altered perception, or crisis in some people.

B. Frequent high-THC exposure may increase risk for some psychosis-related outcomes
   in some vulnerable groups.

C. Continued cannabis use after onset may worsen course or relapse risk for some people.

D. Emerging distress, insomnia, salience change, trauma activation, reward surge,
   mood elevation, or early psychosis may change cannabis use before diagnosis.

E. CUD may mark heavy exposure, dependence, impairment, withdrawal, social cost,
   prior distress, family alarm, service contact, legal pressure, or coded severity.

F. Illegality, stigma, policing, family fear, housing risk, school/work discipline,
   and disclosure risk may alter who is watched, referred, admitted, coded, or remembered.

G. Other substances may carry the pharmacological or social route while cannabis
   receives the record label.

H. Warnings, media, clinician prompts, online search, and family narratives may change
   attention, disclosure, coding, and later data.
```

Those are not one claim.

The rule is:

```text
association != causal object
plant label != exposure chemistry
CUD label != molecular dose
diagnosis code != incidence
service contact != event rate
record order != life order
risk language != mechanism
```

Where causality is asserted from association alone, the result is an unsupported causal leap. Where the evidence supports association but not route ownership, the proper phrase is claimed association. Where the literature or public warning treats the arrow as settled despite live route alternatives, the proper phrase is contested causal claim.

## 2. What survives

A serious object-custody paper must preserve what survives.

The following claims can survive in bounded form:

```text
THC is psychoactive.

Acute cannabis exposure can produce adverse mental-state effects in some people.

Frequent, high-THC, early, or CUD-linked exposure states often travel with stronger
psychosis-related signals.

Some people are more vulnerable than others.

Continued use after psychosis onset may matter for course, relapse, or recovery.

Public-health caution may be justified for bounded exposure routes.
```

Those claims are enough to make CAN-PSY look serious.

They are not enough to make CAN-PSY one object.

A bounded route may win. The public arrow may still lose.

## 4. Why other substances cannot be left in the footnotes

Co-drugs are not a small adjustment. They can change the route.

A cannabis-labelled case may contain:

```text
LSD or psychedelic exposure
stimulants
alcohol
synthetic cannabinoids
tobacco and nicotine
benzodiazepines
antidepressant or antipsychotic changes
withdrawal states
sleep deprivation plus substances
unknown product substitution
```

A special problem is social-stain substitution. Some substances carry heavier social, family, school, work, police, or clinical penalties than others. A person may disclose cannabis and hide LSD. A family may remember cannabis and not know about stimulants. A clinician may record the admitted substance and miss the hidden one. A service file may preserve the least costly or most available explanation.

That creates a stain-gradient route:

```text
less hidden substance label
+ more hidden substance exposure
+ crisis or strange behaviour
+ family/service/police contact
=> cannabis-labelled psychosis record
```

This does not prove cannabis was irrelevant. It blocks the unsupported causal leap from visible label to active route.

No subgroup, culture, gender, class, or place should be treated as a pure cannabis-only exposure class without direct measurement. “No other drugs” is not a demographic fact. It is an empirical burden.

## 5. Why psychosis is not one endpoint

“Psychosis” can mean:

```text
acute intoxication-linked paranoia
panic with threat interpretation
psychotic-like experience
altered salience without service contact
sleep-loss paranoia
clinical high-risk state
brief crisis
first episode psychosis
hospital admission
relapse
cannabis-induced psychotic disorder
psychosis not otherwise specified
schizophrenia-spectrum diagnosis
police-routed crisis
family-routed crisis
administrative code
lived altered state
```

A paper can be right about one endpoint and wrong when the headline inherits all endpoints.

The endpoint needs the same custody tax as the exposure:

```text
what happened?
when did it begin?
how long did it last?
who saw it?
who recorded it?
what route took it into service?
what changed after sleep, cessation, safety, time, or support?
what did the person say it meant?
was impairment measured?
was recurrence measured?
was diagnosis stable?
```

A diagnosis code is a record object. A hospital admission is a contact object. A lived state is a person-state object. They can overlap. They are not interchangeable.

## 6. Why CAN-PSY looks so persuasive

CAN-PSY looks real because many routes feed the same public edge.

```text
Route A: chemical route
  product chemistry + dose + route + tolerance + sleep + vulnerability + co-drugs
  => acute symptoms or increased transition/course risk.

Route B: reverse-regulation route
  early salience, anxiety, insomnia, trauma activation, pain, mood elevation,
  social defeat, or prodromal change
  => cannabis use for sleep, appetite, belonging, fear dampening, pleasure,
     return, brake, or meaning control
  => later service contact or diagnosis.

Route C: shared-liability route
  bullying, trauma, poverty, exclusion, family history, neurodevelopment,
  racism, social defeat, unstable housing, peer context, and co-drugs
  => higher likelihood of cannabis use
  => higher likelihood of psychosis-related outcomes.

Route D: enforcement/contact route
  illegal or stigmatised use
  => secrecy, stress, policing, school/work discipline, housing threat,
     family alarm, delayed help-seeking, record creation
  => cannabis label and psychosis label meet in data.

Route E: service-contact route
  CUD, crisis, family fear, dual-diagnosis clinic, admission, discharge coding,
  chart inheritance
  => records where both labels appear.

Route F: diagnostic-capture route
  known cannabis use
  => different questions, interpretations, differential diagnosis,
     coding practice, or repeated chart language.

Route G: co-drug attribution route
  hidden LSD, stimulants, alcohol, synthetic cannabinoids, medication change,
  or withdrawal
  => visible cannabis label carries more of the record than it earned.

Route H: peak-valence route
  win, romance, recognition, party, creative breakthrough, birth, relief,
  spiritual opening, status shift, or wanted threshold
  + sleep break, body cost, witness-field change, cannabis nearby
  => cannabis-labelled crisis route.

Route I: warning loop
  public warning, media, clinician prompt, family search, self-monitoring
  => increased attention, report, disclosure, coding, and repetition.
```

All nine routes can be real. That is exactly why one-name CAN-PSY fails.

## 7. Three routes that must not impersonate one another

### 7.1 Chemical route

A chemical route says:

```text
specific product chemistry
+ specific exposure pattern
+ specific person-state
+ specific timing
=> specific psychosis-related outcome
```

This route needs product identity, dose, THC/CBD balance, route, frequency, duration, tolerance, reintroduction, sleep, co-drugs, prior symptoms, and endpoint identity.

### 7.2 Enforcement/contact route

An enforcement/contact route says:

```text
illegal or stigmatised cannabis label
+ unequal surveillance, fear, discipline, disclosure risk, service routing,
  police contact, family alarm, or coding practice
=> greater co-visibility of cannabis and psychosis labels
```

This route needs law field, policing, school/work discipline, housing/employment risk, stigma, service path, admission route, and diagnostic practice.

### 7.3 Chemical route inside enforcement/contact field

The mixed route says:

```text
product chemistry may matter,
but the measured relation is shaped by illegality, stigma, contact, service access,
diagnostic expectation, and record visibility
```

This is probably the common field route. It is also the easiest route to misread because both sides are partly true.

The paper must not force a false choice:

```text
not only chemistry
not only law
often chemistry inside law/contact measurement
```

## 8. Peak valence as a gate, not a crown

Peak valence is not a replacement explanation. It is a missing route variable.

Some episodes do not begin only in misery, defeat, fear, or decline. They may follow:

```text
win
recognition
romance
birth
promotion
public success
creative breakthrough
spiritual opening
survival
release
party
relief
status threshold
long-wanted arrival
```

High-valence states can alter:

```text
sleep
appetite
reward drive
sexual or social exposure
identity
obligation
meaning
body cost
visibility
witness response
substance use
family interpretation
service route
```

Cannabis may appear there as:

```text
celebration object
belonging object
reward extender
creative-work object
sleep-after-win attempt
appetite regulator
fear dampener
grounding attempt
return-path object
habit object
```

The gate asks:

```text
Was cannabis a driver of the episode,
a participant in an already-rising state,
a response to the state,
or a visible label attached after the route had already moved?
```

Peak valence does not make the episode harmless. It prevents the wrong variable from taking ownership of the route.

## 9. Movement gates

A claim earns more trust when it says what would move it.

### 9.1 What would strengthen a bounded product-route claim

```text
measured THC and CBD
route and dose per event
frequency and duration
age of initiation
tolerance and withdrawal
reintroduction after abstinence
product source stability
synthetic/adulterant screen
sleep tracking
co-drug measurement
medication changes
pre-use symptoms
pre-escalation symptoms
time to endpoint
endpoint specificity
negative controls
sensitivity tests for reverse causation
subgroup custody
```

A bounded product-route claim can win without granting the public arrow.

### 9.2 What would strengthen an enforcement/contact-route claim

```text
police contact
school/work discipline
housing or employment threat
legal status
stigma burden
disclosure risk
family surveillance
route into services
clinician suspicion
diagnostic wording
chart inheritance
comparison across policy/contact regimes
negative-control outcomes sharing contact routes
```

A contact-route claim wins only if it shows that visibility moved in ways chemistry alone does not explain.

### 9.3 What would strengthen a reverse-path claim

```text
symptoms before cannabis escalation
sleep loss before cannabis escalation
distress before use change
mood elevation before use change
pain or appetite need before use change
witness reports before diagnosis
why the person used cannabis
what changed after use stopped
what changed after sleep recovered
```

Record order is not enough. Symptom order is required.

### 9.4 What would strengthen a co-drug attribution claim

```text
direct measurement of alcohol, stimulants, psychedelics, synthetic cannabinoids,
tobacco, medications, and withdrawal

toxicology where ethically and practically available

private disclosure routes that reduce social-stain underreporting

timeline of all substances before the state changed

comparison between recorded substance and suspected substance

sensitivity analysis for hidden co-use
```

The question is not whether cannabis was present. The question is what role it played relative to the whole exposure field.

### 9.5 What would strengthen a peak-valence route

```text
event before state change
sleep before and after the event
body cost
meaning at the time
witness field
social elevation or status shift
party rhythm
romance, recognition, relief, or win
cannabis role in the state
whether use was celebration, sleep, appetite, fear, belonging, brake, or habit
what worsened, helped, repeated, or stopped
```

Peak valence becomes evidence only when timed and described. Otherwise it is just a useful warning about missing variables.

## 10. Object-custody scorecard

Score each gate:

```text
0 = absent
1 = named but thin
2 = measured with usable detail
3 = measured with timing and sensitivity analysis
```

| Gate                | Question                                                                               | Failure if absent                                 |
| ------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| Product identity    | THC, CBD, ratio, product, route, source, adulterant/synthetic screen?                  | Plant label impersonates chemistry.               |
| Exposure pattern    | Dose, frequency, duration, age, tolerance, withdrawal, reintroduction?                 | Any-use impersonates exposure state.              |
| Co-drug field       | Alcohol, stimulants, psychedelics, nicotine, medication, withdrawal?                   | Cannabis inherits other exposures.                |
| Time order          | What changed before use, heavy use, first contact, and diagnosis?                      | Record order impersonates life order.             |
| Prior state         | Sleep, distress, salience, anxiety, mood elevation, trauma, early symptoms?            | Reverse path vanishes.                            |
| Peak valence        | Win, relief, recognition, love, party, status shift, meaning surge?                    | Reward route vanishes.                            |
| Shared liability    | Bullying, trauma, poverty, exclusion, family history, neurodevelopment, peer context?  | Confound stack becomes drug effect.               |
| Enforcement/contact | Police, court, school/work discipline, stigma, housing/employment threat?              | Law field vanishes.                               |
| Service route       | Help-seeking, referral, admission, clinician suspicion, chart wording?                 | Contact becomes incidence.                        |
| Diagnostic capture  | Does known cannabis use alter questions, interpretation, coding, or chart inheritance? | Diagnosis looks neutral when it is routed.        |
| Outcome identity    | Acute symptom, PLE, FEP, admission, relapse, diagnosis code, schizophrenia?            | Endpoint bucket swallows all.                     |
| Population check    | Do use, potency, policy, diagnosis, admission, and incidence trends move as predicted? | Clinic signal becomes population claim.           |
| Negative controls   | Comparator exposures, outcomes, groups, or policy shifts?                              | Contact route may mimic drug route.               |
| Subgroup custody    | Which people, ages, histories, vulnerabilities, products, and settings?                | Subgroup risk becomes universal slogan.           |
| Return path         | What helped, harmed, repeated, or stopped after the crisis?                            | Recovery evidence gets confused with onset route. |

A paper should report two scores:

```text
A. single CAN-PSY object score
B. bounded route score
```

This prevents an important association from being treated as useless just because it does not prove the whole public arrow.

## 11. Worked audit A: CUD registry paper

### 11.1 Study object

A registry study linking cannabis use disorder records with later schizophrenia diagnoses or incident schizophrenia diagnosis records.

### 11.2 What it strongly supports

```text
CUD records and schizophrenia diagnosis records can co-appear at scale.

The relation is serious enough to require explanation.

CUD is not a harmless administrative label.

Policy period, service access, diagnosis practice, and changing exposure fields
should be investigated together.
```

### 11.3 What it cannot carry alone

```text
CUD != THC dose
CUD != product identity
CUD != chemical exposure by itself
schizophrenia diagnosis record != population incidence by itself
registry order != symptom order
policy period != product chemistry
```

A CUD registry paper may contain:

```text
heavier exposure
dependence
withdrawal
distress regulation
prior symptoms
family alarm
service contact
legal pressure
diagnostic attention
coded severity
poverty
housing instability
co-drug use
```

Those are not noise. They are possible routes.

### 11.4 Dual score

```text
Single CAN-PSY causal-object score:
  8 / 20

Association/contact-field score:
  16 / 20
```

### 11.5 Claim after audit

Strong claim:

```text
CUD registry status is a serious signal for later schizophrenia-diagnosis records
and should trigger route-level investigation.
```

Unsupported causal leap:

```text
Cannabis chemistry caused schizophrenia at the population level.
```

Better route name:

```text
CUD-CONTACT-SCHZ-RECORD
```

Not pretty. More honest.

## 12. Worked audit B: high-potency daily-use FEP case-control paper

### 12.1 Study object

A first-episode psychosis case-control study comparing cannabis exposure patterns, including daily use and high-potency use, between cases and controls.

### 12.2 What it strongly supports

```text
Daily use and high-potency use can be associated with FEP service-contact cases.

Gradient-shaped findings matter.

Product class matters more than any-use.

A bounded high-potency / frequent-use route deserves serious investigation.
```

### 12.3 What it cannot carry alone

```text
high potency category != measured THC/CBD dose
case-control contact route != population incidence
FEP service contact != all psychosis-related states
recorded use before diagnosis != symptom order
daily use != motive, sleep state, co-drug field, or prior vulnerability
```

The study may support a bounded route. It does not grant the whole public object.

### 12.4 Dual score

```text
Single CAN-PSY causal-object score:
  12 / 20

Bounded high-potency / daily-use FEP-route score:
  16.5 / 20
```

### 12.5 Claim after audit

Strong claim:

```text
Daily high-potency cannabis exposure is a serious candidate route for FEP service-contact risk,
especially where product class, frequency, and site differences are visible.
```

Unsupported causal leap:

```text
Cannabis as a whole causes psychosis as a whole.
```

Better route name:

```text
HPD-FEP-CONTACT
```

High-potency daily-use first-episode service-contact route.

## 13. What the two audits show together

The registry paper is strong for association/contact-field seriousness and weak for product-level causality.

The FEP case-control paper is stronger for a bounded high-potency daily-use route and still weak for a universal CAN-PSY object.

Together they show:

```text
claimed association survives
bounded routes may survive
CUD is a trap object
contact is not incidence
product class matters
co-drugs remain load-bearing
public arrow still overclaims
```

The right output is not denial. The right output is route naming.

## 14. Claim-status ladder

Use this ladder instead of the public arrow.

| Level | Claim                                                                              | Status                                                  |
| ----- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------- |
| 0     | No relation between cannabis and psychosis-related outcomes.                       | Too weak. The claimed association has serious evidence. |
| 1     | Cannabis labels and psychosis labels co-appear in studies and records.             | Supported.                                              |
| 2     | Some cannabis exposure states are associated with some psychosis-related outcomes. | Supported in bounded form.                              |
| 3     | Frequent high-THC exposure may be a risk route for some people and endpoints.      | Plausible bounded route.                                |
| 4     | CUD predicts schizophrenia diagnosis because of THC dose.                          | Unsupported without route separation.                   |
| 5     | Cannabis causes psychosis.                                                         | Contested causal claim.                                 |
| 6     | Cannabis as one exposure causes psychosis as one endpoint by one settled route.    | Unsupported causal leap.                                |

The best current phrase is:

```text
Some cannabis exposure states are associated with some psychosis-related outcomes,
but CAN-PSY has not earned trust as one causal object.
```

## 15. Study design for a stronger test

A stronger study would not ask only:

```text
did the person use cannabis?
```

It would ask:

```text
which product?
which route?
which dose?
which person-state?
which co-drugs?
which sleep state?
which timing?
which legal/contact field?
which service route?
which diagnostic route?
which endpoint?
which comparator?
```

Minimum design:

```text
prospective cohort or high-quality registry linkage

product-level cannabis measurement where possible

THC/CBD ratio or best available proxy

dose, route, frequency, duration, age, tolerance, withdrawal, reintroduction

co-drug and medication module, including alcohol, tobacco, stimulants,
psychedelics, synthetic cannabinoids, prescribed medicines, and withdrawal

sleep and circadian tracking

pre-exposure and pre-escalation symptoms

peak-valence and witness-field module

bullying, trauma, poverty, exclusion, social defeat, family history,
neurodevelopment, and peer-context measures

law/contact/service-route fields

diagnostic-route audit

endpoint separation

negative-control exposures and outcomes

population-trend comparison

sensitivity analysis for reverse path, shared liability, co-drug hiding,
and contact visibility
```

Analysis rule:

```text
Do not let the largest available proxy carry the route.
```

## 16. Public wording

Bad public wording:

```text
Cannabis causes psychosis.
```

Better public wording:

```text
Some cannabis exposure patterns, especially frequent high-THC use, are associated
with higher risk of some psychosis-related outcomes. The risk is not the same for
all people, products, settings, or endpoints. Other drugs, prior distress, sleep,
trauma, poverty, exclusion, policing, stigma, service contact, and diagnosis
practice must be separated before making causal claims.
```

Short public wording:

```text
The association is serious. The slogan is too simple.
```

Clinical wording:

```text
For some people, especially those with prior psychosis, strong vulnerability,
sleep instability, heavy use, high-THC exposure, or difficult reactions, avoiding
or reducing cannabis may be protective. That advice does not prove that cannabis
as one object causes psychosis as one object.
```

Research wording:

```text
Report the route. Do not donate missing product, timing, co-drug, contact,
or endpoint detail to CAN-PSY.
```

## 17. Where this paper could be wrong

This paper would weaken if strong studies repeatedly showed:

```text
measured THC/CBD exposure
clear dose and route
minimal co-drug ambiguity
strong symptom-order evidence
pre-use baseline stability
strong control for bullying, trauma, poverty, exclusion, family history,
neurodevelopment, sleep, medication, and other substances
little contact or diagnostic-capture distortion
specific endpoint movement
population movement in the predicted direction
negative controls behaving as expected
replication across settings
```

If those conditions held, a bounded product-route claim should move upward.

Even then, the winning object would not be:

```text
cannabis -> psychosis
```

It would be something closer to:

```text
specific THC-heavy exposure pattern
+ specific vulnerability and state conditions
+ specific timing
=> increased risk for specific psychosis-related endpoint
```

That would be a real route. It would still not be the whole public arrow.

## 18. Conclusion

CAN-PSY looks real because real routes converge.

The mistake is not taking cannabis risk seriously. The mistake is letting one public phrase inherit every route that pushes cannabis labels and psychosis labels into the same records.

The paper’s final position is:

```text
The claimed association may survive.
Bounded product routes may survive.
CUD/contact routes may survive.
Enforcement and diagnostic routes may survive.
Reverse, shared-liability, co-drug, and peak-valence routes may survive.

The single CAN-PSY object has not survived.
```

The association is serious.

The slogan is not the object.

A route can win only after it names what moved.
