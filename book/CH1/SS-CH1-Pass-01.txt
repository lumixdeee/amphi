P1: CONTENT (Claims Extracted, No Evaluation)

Core claims:

The common picture of work overemphasizes creation and underemphasizes maintenance.
A significant portion of organizational work is the maintenance of meaning rather than the production of artifacts.
Goals frequently undergo gradual transformation while retaining their original labels.
This transformation often occurs without malice or conscious intent.
Many apparently distinct failure modes (requirements drift, scope creep, changing definitions, stakeholder disagreement, etc.) may be manifestations of a common underlying process.
Meaning can enter a system and leave a system in a non-identical form.
Recovering, translating, and repairing damaged meaning constitutes a large amount of hidden labor.
Many professional roles can be understood as semantic repair functions.
Semantic maintenance costs increase with organizational scale.
Large language models expose semantic transformation processes because they perform rapid representational changes.
Human institutions and language models both substitute neighboring objectives for original objectives.
Existing fields focus more on creating meaning than preserving it.
Civilization may devote substantial resources to repairing semantic damage.
Semantic maintenance deserves treatment as an independent discipline.
The central question is not merely how ideas are created, but how they are preserved.

Critical Supports

The book's framework appears to depend on three supports:

Support 1: Meaning can be tracked

If meaning cannot be tracked across transformations, semantic maintenance becomes immeasurable.

Support 2: Drift is detectable

If all reinterpretation is considered normal evolution, there is no maintenance problem.

Support 3: Preservation can be distinguished from adaptation

Without this distinction:

every change becomes acceptable
or
every change becomes failure

The framework needs a middle category.

Blind Spots
Blind Spot 1: Drift versus learning

The chapter currently treats change and loss as closely related.

A future reader may ask:

How do we distinguish semantic damage from genuine improvement?

Example:

Original request:
"make users safe"

Later refinement:
"reduce a specific failure mode"

That may be preservation through clarification rather than drift.

The framework will need a criterion.

Blind Spot 2: Original intent may be internally inconsistent

The opening example assumes a coherent original object.

In practice many requests contain:

contradictions
ambiguity
unstated tradeoffs

Sometimes later transformations are attempts to resolve those conflicts rather than semantic damage.

Blind Spot 3: Preservation may not be the primary objective

Organizations sometimes intentionally trade fidelity for:

speed
scale
coordination
legal defensibility

The framework may need to explain why semantic loss occurs even when participants understand the loss.

Blind Spot 4: Meaning is treated as singular

The chapter often speaks as if:

meaning entered the system

Yet many organizational situations begin with multiple meanings simultaneously.

Different stakeholders may already carry different objects before any drift occurs.

Adversarial Review

Strongest challenge to the chapter:

The proposed unifying mechanism may be too powerful.

Many introductory frameworks become attractive because they explain everything.

A skeptical reader could argue:

scope creep is not semantic drift
incentive problems are not semantic drift
political conflict is not semantic drift
principal-agent failures are not semantic drift

To survive this challenge, the book will eventually need to show either:

these phenomena are genuinely downstream of semantic maintenance failures,

or

semantic maintenance is a necessary component within them.

Without that demonstration, the framework risks becoming a universal explanatory solvent.

What Changed?

Initial state:

Work appears to be creation.

Final state:

Creation is visible, but a substantial fraction of work may actually be preserving meaning as representations move through systems.

Δ:

The object of attention shifts from production to preservation.

That is the chapter's primary contribution. It does not yet prove the thesis, but it successfully establishes a coherent question:

Once an idea exists, what exactly must remain invariant for us to say the same idea survived?
